# Creative Brief Guide

The creative brief is the campaign's north star. Every post, image, and caption should trace back to it. This guide shows how to write briefs that produce cohesive, high-quality campaigns.

---

## What Makes a Great Campaign Concept?

A campaign concept is ONE idea that unifies everything. It's not a tagline, not a slogan, not a value proposition — it's the creative thesis that every piece of content expresses from a different angle.

### The Test: Specificity

A concept must be specific to THIS product. Apply the competitor swap test:

> If you can replace the brand name with a competitor's name and the concept still works, it's too generic. Start over.

**Generic (fails the test):**
- "Innovation that matters" — could be literally any tech company
- "Your trusted partner" — could be a bank, a law firm, or a tire company
- "Simple, powerful, beautiful" — Apple, Notion, and 10,000 other brands

**Specific (passes the test):**
- **1Password:** "Your memory is for moments, not passwords" — only works for a password manager
- **Proofly:** "In a world where seeing isn't believing, proof is power" — only works for a verification tool
- **Duolingo:** "The guilt trip that actually teaches you Spanish" — only works for a gamified language app
- **Notion:** "One workspace that thinks the way you do" — specific to Notion's flexible structure

### How to Develop the Concept

Start with the Brand Narrative from Phase 1, then work through these prompts:

1. **The Tension Reframe:** Take the product's core problem and express it as a human truth, not a technical problem.
   - Technical: "AI-generated images are hard to detect"
   - Human truth: "We've entered an era where your own eyes can betray you"

2. **The Emotional Core:** What emotion sits at the center of the product experience?
   - Fear? (security products)
   - Relief? (productivity tools)
   - Joy? (creative tools)
   - Empowerment? (education products)
   - Belonging? (community products)

3. **The Cultural Moment:** What's happening in the world right now that makes this product relevant?
   - "AI is everywhere, and so is AI-generated content that looks real"
   - "Remote work made collaboration tools essential, not optional"
   - "Data breaches are in the news every week"

4. **The Concept Formula:** Combine the tension, emotion, and cultural moment:
   > In a world where [cultural moment], [brand name] gives you [emotional resolution] through [unique mechanism].

   Then distill that into one sharp sentence.

## Example Creative Briefs

### Example 1: SaaS / AI Detection Tool

```markdown
# Creative Brief: Proofly — "Truth Has a New Tool"

## Campaign Concept
"In a world where seeing isn't believing, proof is power."

## Campaign Narrative Arc
- Act 1 (Posts 1-2): THE PROBLEM IS PERSONAL
  Open with content that makes the audience realize THEY can't tell real from AI.
  Don't mention Proofly. Let the problem land first.

- Act 2 (Posts 3-4): THE PROBLEM IS BIGGER THAN YOU THINK
  Show scale — statistics, real-world examples of manipulation, consequences.
  The audience should feel: "This is scary and getting worse."

- Act 3 (Posts 5-6): THE ANSWER EXISTS
  Introduce Proofly — not as a product pitch, but as an inevitable response.
  "Someone had to build this. We did."

- Act 4 (Posts 7-8): HOW IT WORKS
  Product deep-dives that feel earned. The audience now understands WHY it matters,
  so features become meaningful, not just checkboxes.

- Act 5 (Posts 9-10): YOU'RE NOT ALONE
  Social proof, community, Product Hunt feature. The message: join the movement.

## Visual Identity System
- Hero Element: A "verification lens" — a circular magnifying/scanning element
  that appears in different forms across posts
- Color Usage: Deep navy backgrounds for "the problem" posts, lighter backgrounds
  with green accents for "the solution" posts — the palette itself tells the story
- Typography: Large, bold numbers for statistics. Clean sans-serif for everything else.
  Headlines never exceed 6 words.
- Recurring Motif: Split-screen / dual-reality compositions (real vs AI, before vs after,
  uncertain vs verified)
- Visual Don'ts: No generic tech gradients, no illustrations of "AI brains," no stock photos

## Concept-to-Post Mapping
| Post | Campaign Role | Emotional Beat | Visual Hook | Caption Hook |
|------|--------------|----------------|-------------|--------------|
| Mon W1 | Act 1 - Wake up | Shock, unease | Two identical photos — one real, one AI | "One of these is real. One is AI." |
| Tue W1 | Act 1 - Make it personal | Vulnerability | A shared viral image with "UNVERIFIED" stamp | "You shared it because it felt true." |
| Thu W1 | Act 2 - Scale the problem | Urgency | Bold statistic — 84% in large type | "84% of viral conflict images show manipulation" |
| Fri W1 | Act 2 - Real consequences | Alarm | Split: "What you saw" vs "What was real" | "This photo changed public opinion. It was generated." |
| Sat W1 | Act 3 - Solution intro | Relief, curiosity | Proofly UI revealing truth — scan in progress | "3 seconds. That's all it takes." |
| Mon W2 | Act 3 - How it works | Empowerment | Step-by-step: Send → Scan → Know | "You don't need an app. Just WhatsApp." |
| Tue W2 | Act 4 - Feature deep-dive | Trust | Verification result card — detailed confidence score | "We don't guess. We prove." |
| Thu W2 | Act 4 - Behind the tech | Admiration | Clean data viz — 2.4M files analyzed | "We analyzed 2.4 million files to train this." |
| Fri W2 | Act 5 - Social proof | Belonging | Product Hunt feature badge + celebration | "Featured on Product Hunt. The movement grows." |
| Sat W2 | Act 5 - Community challenge | Fun, engagement | Interactive quiz — "AI or Real?" | "Think you can tell? Let's find out." |
```

### Example 2: E-Commerce / DTC Brand

```markdown
# Creative Brief: Soja & Co. — "Scent Is the Shortcut to Stillness"

## Campaign Concept
"In a world that won't stop buzzing, scent is the shortcut to stillness."

## Campaign Narrative Arc
- Act 1 (Posts 1-2): THE OVERSTIMULATION PROBLEM
  Show the chaos — screens, notifications, mental clutter. No product.

- Act 2 (Posts 3-4): THE CRAVING FOR CALM
  Tap into the universal craving for slowness. Mindfulness without the app.

- Act 3 (Posts 5-6): SCENT AS A RITUAL
  Introduce candles — not as products, but as a ritual. "Lighting a candle
  is the simplest act of creating space."

- Act 4 (Posts 7-8): THE CRAFT
  Behind-the-scenes of hand-pouring, ingredient sourcing. The product earns
  respect through process.

- Act 5 (Posts 9-10): YOUR RITUAL, YOUR SPACE
  UGC, customer stories, styled environments. The message: make it yours.

## Visual Identity System
- Hero Element: A soft, diffused flame glow — present in every post
- Color Usage: Warm neutrals (cream, sand, clay) as primary. Deep forest
  green accent. Never stark white backgrounds. Every post should feel warm.
- Typography: Thin, elegant serif for headlines. No all-caps screaming.
- Recurring Motif: "Negative space as calm" — generous whitespace that FEELS
  like the stillness the product promises.
- Visual Don'ts: No busy lifestyle flat-lays, no aggressive sales graphics,
  no text over busy backgrounds.
```

### Example 3: Personal Brand / Creator

```markdown
# Creative Brief: Dev Creator — "Build in Public, Learn in Public"

## Campaign Concept
"The best way to learn to code is to let the internet watch you struggle."

## Campaign Narrative Arc
- Act 1 (Posts 1-2): THE IMPOSTER SYNDROME HOOK
  Tap into the universal developer fear of being "found out."

- Act 2 (Posts 3-4): THE PERMISSION TO BE BAD
  Challenge the polished tutorial culture. Show real, messy, first-draft code.

- Act 3 (Posts 5-6): THE BUILD
  Show what you're building this week. Warts and all. Comments welcome.

- Act 4 (Posts 7-8): THE LESSON
  Extract a universal principle from this week's specific struggles.

- Act 5 (Posts 9-10): THE COMMUNITY
  Feature followers' projects, answer questions, celebrate wins.

## Visual Identity System
- Hero Element: A glowing cursor/caret on a dark editor background
- Color Usage: Dark mode always. Code syntax colors as accent palette.
  Green for success, amber for challenges, red for bugs.
- Typography: Monospace for code, clean sans-serif for commentary.
- Recurring Motif: "The terminal" — framing content as if it's happening
  inside a code editor.
- Visual Don'ts: No polished "tech influencer" staging. No stock photos.
  Everything should feel like it came from an actual developer's screen.
```

## Common Creative Brief Mistakes

### 1. The Non-Concept
"We want to create engaging content that showcases our product and drives awareness."

This isn't a concept — it's a job description. A concept is an IDEA, not an objective.

### 2. The Feature Masquerading as a Concept
"Proofly uses advanced AI to detect generated images with 94% accuracy."

This is a feature, not a concept. Features answer "what." Concepts answer "why should I care?"

### 3. The Generic Emotional Appeal
"Empowering people to make better decisions."

Could be a bank, a nutrition app, or a horoscope site. Specificity is everything.

### 4. The Missing Narrative Arc
Having a great concept but no story progression. If every post says the same thing from a different angle, the audience gets bored by Day 3. The narrative must BUILD.

### 5. The Visual Afterthought
Defining the concept and narrative but leaving visuals as "use brand colors and make it look nice." The Visual Identity System is what makes a campaign recognizable as a campaign — not just a collection of posts.

## Concept Validation Checklist

Before proceeding to content generation, verify:

- [ ] **Competitor Swap Test:** Replace brand name with top competitor — does the concept still work? If yes, revise.
- [ ] **The Dinner Party Test:** Can you explain the concept to a friend in one sentence without jargon? If not, simplify.
- [ ] **The "So What?" Test:** After reading the concept, would a stranger in the target audience say "interesting" or "so what?" It must be the former.
- [ ] **The 10-Post Test:** Can you imagine 10 different posts that all express this concept from different angles? If you run out of ideas at Post 3, the concept is too narrow.
- [ ] **The Emotional Test:** Does the concept make you FEEL something? Not "professional" — actually feel something. If not, dig deeper.
- [ ] **The Arc Test:** Does the narrative build? Does Post 10 feel different from Post 1? If the arc is flat, restructure it.
